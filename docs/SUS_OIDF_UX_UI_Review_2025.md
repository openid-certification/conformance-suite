Context

The OpenID Foundation plays a central role in enabling secure, interoperable digital identity
& API access across global, regulated ecosystems. As adoption of OpenID Foundation
standards accelerates, OIDF is entering a new phase of organisational maturity.

The move toward the Accredited Labs & Partners Model reﬂects the need to scale
conformance & certiﬁcation beyond a centralised approach that focuses on those with testing
experience.

This transition brings new expectations of clarity, usability, & resilience across OIDF’s tooling &
documentation, as responsibilities are increasingly distributed to a wider & more diverse set
of users from labs, ecosystems, & implementers operating under varying regulatory &
operational conditions.

The 5 organisational contexts in our research

We identiﬁed 5 broad organisational contexts across our testing. While by no
means exhaustive these contexts do provide useful insights about current use cases
for the CTS.

A. The Intermediary / Identity Network Provider (ConnectID)

B. The Ecosystem Provider / Validator (Raidiam)

C. The Mandated Bank (NatWest, Nationwide)

D. The Regulator / Standards Body (Open Banking)

E. The Early Adopter / Implementation Tester (Mastercard, FIME)

© Super User Studio Ltd.

Implementer

A. The Intermediary / Identity Network Provider
(ConnectID)

The Offering: ConnectID provides a digital identity network connecting Identity Providers (banks) with Relying Parties (merchants)
using SDKs.

Role of the CTS: It acts as a "certiﬁcation of testing" for both the SDK and the Relying Party (RP) implementations. It ensures RPs
haven't altered the SDK implementation in a way that breaks compliance.

Problem Solved: Increases the level of assurance and trust between B2B partners by proving adherence to FAPI 2 standards.

Who is Involved:

● Tech Sales: Manages the relationships with RPs, guides clients through the process, debugs high-level issues.

●

Software Engineers: Automate the CTS internally via API to test the conformance of nightly builds of their SDK; handles
complex debugging when client conformance fails.

● Client Developers: Varying skill levels (from enterprise teams to "half a developer" at a startup) who actually run the tests.

When Things Go Wrong: Tech sales compare client logs against known "good" logs. If unresolved, it escalates to engineering. Clients
often fail due to conﬁguration errors or not reading the customised documentation that ConnectID provide.

Post-Conformance: A complex administrative process involving coupons, payment codes, and multiple forms. ConnectID often
absorbs the cost for smaller clients.

© Super User Studio Ltd.

Implementer / Accredited lab

B. The Ecosystem Provider / Validator (Raidiam)

The Offering: Provides trust frameworks and directories for Open Finance and Open Insurance ecosystems (e.g.
in Brazil). They offer a "forked" version of the OIDF suite to their customers.

Role of the CTS: It’s used to run regulatory tests for ecosystem participants. They run it against their own Mock
Bank to validate tests, and participants run it to validate their APIs.

Problem Solved: Validates speciﬁc business rules and Swagger speciﬁcations deﬁned by the Central Bank,
ensuring interoperability across the ecosystem.

Who is Involved:

● QA Engineers: Run tests against new plans/updates; validate results manually against requirements

● Product & Dev Teams: Write the test plans and ﬁx code when tests fail.

When Things Go Wrong: QA analyses logs to distinguish between conﬁguration errors, mock bank errors, or
implementation errors. They rely on internal Conﬂuence pages for conﬁguration data.

Post-Conformance: Participants submit a public link to their test results. Raidiam uses automation to scan the
logs; if compliant, a certiﬁcate is issued automatically.

© Super User Studio Ltd.

Implementer

C. The Mandated Bank / ASPSP* (NatWest, Nationwide)

The Offering: Major UK banks providing Open Banking APIs.

Role of the CTS: Mandatory annual re-certiﬁcation to comply with CMA9/Open Banking regulations.

Problem Solved: Regulatory compliance (FAPI) .

Who is Involved:

● Solutions Specialists/Engineers: Deeply technical staff who maintain the local environments and run the

tests,.

When Things Go Wrong: Disputes can arise over interpretation of specs (e.g. capitalization of ‘Bearer’ headers
or speciﬁc error messages from third-party IAM providers).

Post-Conformance:

● NatWest: Payment and submission handled by business/payment teams; requires internal sign-off.

● Nationwide: Logs attached to Jira tickets as evidence; separate team handles formal submission.

* Account Servicing Payment Service Provider

© Super User Studio Ltd.

UK Ecosystem

D. The Regulator / Standards Body (Open Banking)

The Offering: Oversees the CMA9 banks in the UK.

Role of the CTS: They are ‘consumers of the output’. They do not run the suite but check the OIDF certiﬁcation
list to monitor bank compliance.

Problem Solved: Outsourcing the technical validation of security standards (FAPI) to OIDF so they don't have to
build it themselves.

Who is Involved:

●

Standards and QA Leads (Non-operational regarding the suite).

When Things Go Wrong:

●

●

Banks contact them when confused by OIDF versioning or payments. They redirect banks back to OIDF
for support.

They will be responsible for 10x more ﬁnancial institutions in the future, and have genuine concern about
the potentially large step change in support that is required of them (OIDF should be aware of this too!).

Post-Conformance: They manually check the OIDF website for the conformance status of each bank.

© Super User Studio Ltd.

Implementer / Accredited lab

E. The Early Adopter / Implementation Tester
(Mastercard, FIME)

The Offering: FIME is a certiﬁcation lab; Mastercard is implementing OAuth2/FAPI2 platforms.

The Role of the CTS: Mastercard is using it to validate pre-launch implementations of FAPI 2. FIME is exploring it
to become an accredited lab.

Problem Solved: Validating internal implementations against new speciﬁcations where gaps might exist in the
test suite itself.

Who is Involved:

●

Tech Leads/Managers, with extensive knowledge of FAPI standards.

When Things Go Wrong: Direct collaboration with OIDF to ﬁx bugs in the test suite (Mastercard) or strictly
reading documentation (FIME).

1. Organisations are only usually
interested in using the CTS to test
against one or two standards.

Most participants interact with the test suite to satisfy a speciﬁc
regulatory mandate or product requirement. Consequently, they are only
interested in a very small subset (ie. one or two) of the available tests; all
other tests are effectively ‘noise’ to them.

For example:

●

●

In the UK, organizations like NatWest, Nationwide, and OBL are
focused entirely on the speciﬁc proﬁle mandated by the UK
Competition and Markets Authority (FAPI 1.0 Advanced Final).
They do not explore other tests.

Vendors like ConnectID and Mastercard use the suite to certify a
speciﬁc software product or SDK. Once the target proﬁle (e.g.
FAPI 2 Security Proﬁle) is identiﬁed, they have no business reason
to run other tests.

“You know, if you think that
the suite covers quite a
large broad range, we're
only interested in two tests
effectively."

Open Banking

© Super User Studio Ltd.

2. The CTS is used by people
with a wide range of technical
abilities

The CTS is used by people with a wide range of technical abilities. The
user base ranges from ‘Super Users’ who read the source code to
understand the tool, to ‘Process Users’ who ﬁnd the terminology alien
and rely entirely on step-by-step guides.

Super Users - These users understand the underlying speciﬁcations
(FAPI/OIDC) and the code itself. They ﬁnd the tool straightforward or
bypass the UI entirely to build their own automation.

Process Users - These users are technically literate (often QA
engineers) but struggle with the speciﬁc complexity of the CTS. They
ﬁnd the process stressful and rely on bespoke documentation (eg.
internal wikis) to navigate the tool.

"Being from a technical
background and knowing
what's happening behind the
scenes probably really helps...
I could really understand...
someone that might not have
that knowledge... some of
those FAPI errors would be
really like ‘what does that
even mean’?"

Senior Software Engineer
(Nationwide)

"6 months in and I am not still
able to tell what every error is
about"

QA Engineer (Raidiam)

3. OIDF documentation is generally bypassed in
favour of ‘shadow documentation’.

Organisations usually bypassed complex OIDF documentation in favor of internal workarounds to ensure repeatability and
clarity. Examples of such ‘Shadow Documentation’ include:

●

●

Internal Wikis: Organisations (Nationwide, NatWest, Raidiam) maintain private Conﬂuence pages and Wikis to bridge the
gap between OIDF’s extensive documentation and their speciﬁc technical environments. Since certiﬁcation is often an
annual event, internal docs serve as the ‘institutional memory’ that OIDF’s site lacks for speciﬁc setup needs.

Intermediaries as ‘Complexity Shields’:  Organisations like ConnectID and Raidiam act as buffers, rewriting technical
OIDF specs into ‘step-by-step’ guides for the speciﬁc needs of their less technical clients (e.g. web shop developers).

● Certiﬁed Implementations instead of documentation:  Some users (eg. Raidiam) ignore documentation and instead

browse the Certiﬁed Implementations page to copy the parameters of successfully certiﬁed companies.

● UI bypasses: To simplify the process and avoid manual entry errors, users store complex JSON conﬁgurations internally

and simply copy & paste them into the tool.

●

Source Code as Documentation : Extremely high-level technical users (e.g. at Mastercard) ignore all documentation
entirely. Instead, they reverse-engineer the test suite source code to understand test logic, ﬁnding it more
self-explanatory than the written word.

© Super User Studio Ltd.

4. There are trade-offs for local
and cloud hosting of the CTS

Our user research revealed a clear tension between the accessibility
and low maintenance of the cloud version of the CTS versus the
security, connectivity, and early-testing capabilities of the local version.

Local hosting is often mandatory for banks and implementers who
need to test endpoints that are behind corporate ﬁrewalls or in local
development environments. This is efficient in terms of development
time, but adds a burden of maintaining the CTS (which may cause some
organisations to choose to avoid this route).

Cloud hosting has zero maintenance & is much easier for organisations
that can expose their environments (or use SDKs); as such, it’s mainly
used to test and certify production environments.

Some organisations (eg NatWest) run a local version of the CTA for
development but use the Cloud version for the annual certiﬁcation run
of their production site.

"I don't run the OIDF suite
myself locally... it's more
complexity.. more stuff to
manage."

Principal Software
Engineer (ConnectID)

© Super User Studio Ltd.

team sport

● When testing is complete, ﬁnalising certiﬁcation and
payment is handed off to admin/ﬁnance teams.

6. Most errors are conﬁguration
errors, not implementation errors

Participants consistently identify the ‘Setup’ and ‘Conﬁguration’ phases
as the most difficult aspects of the process. Actual code implementation
often works (especially when using SDKs), but getting the test suite to
correctly talk to that implementation requires navigating a complex
mineﬁeld of keys, certiﬁcates, and URLs where errors are frequent.

● When errors occur, considerable effort is spent determining if the
error is a genuine failure of the software (implementation) or
simply a mismatch in the conﬁguration.

●

For intermediaries like ConnectID, their clients use pre-certiﬁed
SDKs. Therefore, the implementation code is technically ‘perfect’
(already veriﬁed). Yet, clients frequently fail tests. These failures
are almost exclusively due to misconﬁguration of the test suite
(e.g., wrong Client IDs, swapped certiﬁcates) rather than actual
software bugs.

“Conﬁguration errors, such as
a wrong variable, would only
be discovered after running
the test when failures occur.”

Customer Support
(NatWest)

“The most challenging
aspect… is the conﬁguration
process required to get the
test plan and all test modules
to pass”

Product / Customer Support
(Raidiam)

●

●

The risk of silos

The connectivity between openid.net and gitlab.com relies almost exclusively on hyperlinks, with no deep
integration or shared navigation systems. The "How to Certify" page links to the Wiki, but there is no uniﬁed
search index that spans both domains.

A user navigating the GitLab Wiki for technical conﬁguration details may struggle to ﬁnd administrative
information regarding payment codes, which resides solely on openid.net. Conversely, a user on openid.net
searching for technical troubleshooting issue will likely fail to retrieve relevant results from the GitLab
repository.

This lack of integration forces the user to maintain two distinct mental models of the certiﬁcation system:
one for Process, Fees and Policy and one for Code, Conﬁguration, Debugging.

As previously observed, conformance is very much a team effort, with some individuals spanning both the
admin and technical side of things. As such, it is recommended that OIDF move to a single, uniﬁed
portal/knowledgebase for conformance help and documentation, where all content is searchable and
tagged according to type and stage of process.

Accessibility: moderate issues

There were three  moderate accessibility issues that occurred across these pages and should be rectiﬁed.

Missing skip navigation links.  There is no "skip to main content" link for keyboard users or <main> landmark
to identify where the main page content begins, meaning that keyboard users must navigate through
repetitive header content. The current site relies heavily on styled <div> elements to create structure, rather
than semantic markup.
Recommendation:  Add skip navigation link at top of page

Insufficient colour contrast. There may be insufficient contrast when grey text appears on grey backgrounds
(eg. for ‘Not run’ or ‘Download logs’ buttons).
Recommendation:  Check all hex values using a contrast checking tool (e.g.
https://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/)

Scalability Restrictions
The viewport meta tag uses user-scalable=no. This prevents visually impaired users from zooming on mobile.
Recommendation:  Consider using <meta name="viewport" content="width=device-width, initial-scale=1.0">
instead.

Why is Linear Docs considered ‘Gold Standard’?

Structural Alignment (It mirrors the product)

IA (Information Architecture)  is most effective when the
mental model of the documentation matches the
mental model of the product and user. Linear’s docs
are organised exactly how the app is built: eg Teams /
Projects / Issues.

High ‘Information Scent’ through labeling

In IA, ‘Information Scent’ refers to the user's ability to guess
what is behind a link based on its label. Linear uses clear,
concise labeling and avoids jargon-heavy internal names in
favor of industry-standard terms. This makes the
documentation accessible for both new users and power
users.

‘Best bets’ for users

Surfacing ‘Popular’ and ‘Linear basics’ content gives most
users what they need immediately.  Those with more
complex needs can use the navigation or search

Progressive disclosure

Linear follows the IA principle of progressive disclosure: giving users only
the information they need at that moment, rather than overwhelming them
with everything at once. The sidebar uses a nested accordion style. You see
high-level categories (eg Basics, Product) ﬁrst. You only see the granular
details once you’ve explored or searched further.

‘Tripartite’ navigation system

Linear employs a classic but perfectly executed three-column layout that
maximizes wayﬁnding:
